# charge: why can it mean bill, power up, and rush?

Q. Why can `charge` mean ask for money, fill a battery, and rush forward?

Many English words have several meanings.
If you look closely, those meanings often connect through one image.
`charge` makes sense when you think of loading something with force.

## 1) Core Link at a Glance

- `charge` = bill
- `charge` = power up
- `charge` = rush

Shared feeling: `load something and push it forward`.

## 2) Step 1: Origin / Form

Historically, `charge` connects to the idea of loading or placing a burden on something.
That helps explain why you can `charge` a cost to an account, `charge` a battery with energy, and `charge` at someone with force.

## 3) Step 2: Key Meanings

### ‚Ď† bill

- This means putting a cost onto someone.
- The link is loading a financial burden onto an account or person.
- English: `They charged me extra for delivery.`
- Meaning: They added an extra cost for delivery.

### ‚Ď° power up

- This means filling a battery with energy.
- The link is loading energy into something.
- English: `I need to charge my phone before I leave.`
- Meaning: I need to fill my phone battery before I leave.

### ‚ĎĘ rush

- This means move forward with force.
- The link is being loaded with energy and pushing ahead.
- English: `The bull charged across the field.`
- Meaning: The bull rushed forward across the field.

- `supercharge` / [ňĆsuňźp…ôrňąt É…Ďňźrd í]

## 5) Wrap-up

The easiest image for `charge` is `load and push`.
If you load money onto a bill, it means charge.
If you load energy into a battery, it means charge.
If you load force into motion, it also means charge.
