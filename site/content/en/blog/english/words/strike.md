# strike: why can it mean hit, stop work, and hit the mind?

Q. Why can `strike` mean hit something, go on strike, and suddenly affect the mind?

Many English words have several meanings.
If you look closely, those meanings often connect through one image.
`strike` is not a perfect one-to-one match in every sense, but the image of sudden impact makes it much easier.

## 1) Core Link at a Glance

- `strike` = hit
- `strike` = stop work in protest
- `strike` = suddenly impress or occur

Shared feeling: `land with force and create impact`.

## 2) Step 1: Origin / Form

`strike` is not a neat prefix-root word.
For learners, the most useful image is simple: `hit sharply`.
That image helps connect physical contact, social pressure, and mental impact.

## 3) Step 2: Key Meanings

### ‚Ď† hit

- This is the basic physical meaning.
- The link is direct force landing on something.
- English: `Lightning struck the tree last night.`
- Meaning: Lightning hit the tree last night.

### ‚Ď° stop work in protest

- Workers can `strike` to put pressure on a company or government.
- The link is creating strong impact by stopping work.
- English: `The workers plan to strike next week.`
- Meaning: The workers plan to stop work in protest next week.

### ‚ĎĘ suddenly impress or occur

- An idea or impression can `strike` you.
- The link is a sudden mental impact.
- English: `His idea struck me as simple and smart.`
- Meaning: His idea hit me as simple and smart.

## 4) Step 3: Related Words / Patterns

- `striker` / [ňąstra…™k…ôr]

Example: `The striker scored in the final minute.`

- `striking` / [ňąstra…™k…™Ňč]

- `strikeout` / [ňąstra…™kňĆa ät]

- `counterstrike` / [ňąka änt…ôrňĆstra…™k]

## 5) Wrap-up

The easiest image for `strike` is `sudden impact`.
If force lands on something, it means hit.
If work stops to create pressure, it means strike.
If an idea lands on your mind, it can also strike you.
